Data classifications

We use security classifications to help us identify and work with sensitive data. Services can be locked down if data is not classified correctly.

OFFICIAL data

Most of the data we create and handle in government is classified as OFFICIAL. This includes routine business operations and services.

Here are some examples of OFFICIAL information:

  • day-to-day business of the public sector, including information about public services and finances
  • routine international relations and diplomatic activities
  • public safety, criminal justice and law enforcement
  • routine defence and security business
  • commercial information, including contractual information and intellectual property
  • personal information that is protected under Data Protection legislation

You do not need to explicitly mark OFFICIAL information.

Check how to store OFFICIAL data.

OFFICIAL-SENSITIVE data

A small amount of OFFICIAL information is of a particularly sensitive nature. This includes information where loss or disclosure would have damaging consequences for your organisation or cause significant distress for an individual or group of people.

Here are some examples of OFFICIAL-SENSITIVE (OS) information:

  • the most sensitive corporate information, such as organisational restructuring, negotiations and major security or business continuity issues
  • very sensitive personal information, such as information about vulnerable or at-risk people
  • policy development and advice to ministers on contentious and very sensitive issues
  • commercially or market sensitive information
  • information about investigations and civil or criminal proceedings that could disrupt law enforcement or prejudice court cases
  • sensitive diplomatic business or international negotiations
You must clearly mark this kind of information as OFFICIAL-SENSITIVE.

Check how to store OFFICIAL-SENSITIVE data.

SECRET and TOP SECRET data

Any information that is very sensitive and requires additional security measures is considered SECRET. For example, any information that could seriously damage military capabilities, international relations or the investigation of serious organised crime.

Any information that requires the highest level of protection from the most serious threats is considered TOP SECRET. For example, any information that could cause widespread loss of life or threaten the security or economic wellbeing of the country or friendly nations.

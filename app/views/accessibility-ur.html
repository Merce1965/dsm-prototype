Accessibility in defence

Before we design or build anything, we need to think about how users access and use services. Accessibility is different from assisted digital, which is helping users with low digital skills or limited access to the web.

Meet the regulations

To meet government accessibility regulations, your digital service must:

  • meet level AA of the Web Content Accessibility Guidelines (WCAG 2.1) as a minimum
  • work on the most commonly used assistive technologies - including screen magnifiers, screen readers and speech recognition tools
  • include disabled people in user research
  • have an accessibility statement that explains how accessible the service is - you need to publish this when the service moves into public beta
Your service must be accessible to everyone who needs it. You may be breaking the law if you do not make it accessible.

Read the Government Digital Service (GDS) guidance on a understanding accessibility requirements for public sector bodies.

Battlespace accessibility needs

Defence has its own set of unique user needs, especially in the deployed or operational space for military users.

Military users in the battle-space might experience accessibility challenges that are not typical compared with other users.These can include issues such as:

  • low to no 3G and 4G connectivity on board vessels
  • the inability to use 2-factor authentication to access online platforms, such as Defence Gateway
  • using restrictive hardware when operating machinery, such as thick gloves
  • using devices with limited battery power, meaning tasks must be completed in as little time as possible

You must also consider accessibility issues related to the battle environment, such as using services in:

  • difficult outdoor conditions, such as using wet screens
  • low light, red light or no light conditions
  • high-noise or low to no-noise circumstances
  • high-stress and high-risk scenarios
  • situations of extreme fatigue
  • extreme atmospheric conditions such as temperatures below freezing, or extreme heat and sunlight

Remember, accessibility needs can be permanent, temporary or situational. This also applies to the military user.

Accessibility statements

You need to publish an accessibility statement to explain how accessible your website or mobile app is.

Most people looking at your statement will not be accessibility experts, so make sure it’s written in plain English that everyone can understand. This will also make it easier for users with a disability to understand how they can best use your website or mobile app.

Your statement needs to cover:

  • whether your website or mobile app is ‘fully’, ‘partially’ or ‘not’ compliant with accessibility standards
  • if it’s not fully compliant, which parts of your website or mobile app do not currently meet accessibility standards and why (for example, because they are exempt or it would be a disproportionate burden to fix things)
  • how people can get alternatives to content that’s not accessible to them
  • how to contact you to report accessibility problems - and a link to the website that they can use if they’re not happy with your response.

GDS has produced a model accessibility statement that you can use for your service.

Things you might not need to fix

Your team does not need to fix the following types of content because they’re exempt from the accessibility regulations:

  • pre-recorded audio and video published before 23 September 2020
  • live audio and video
  • heritage collections like scanned manuscripts
  • PDFs or other documents published before 23 September 2018 - unless users need them to use a service, for example a form that lets you request school meal preferences
  • maps but you’ll need to provide essential information, like an address, in an accessible format
  • third party content that’s under someone else’s control if you did not pay for it or develop it yourself, for example social media ‘like’ buttons
  • content on intranets or extranets published before 23 September 2019 - unless you make a major revision after that date
  • archived websites if they are not needed for services your organisation provides and they are not updated

In your accessibility statement, you need to explain that things like this are not accessible because they are exempt.

Publish accessible documents

Any documents you include in your service, such as PDFs, must be accessible.

GDS has produced guidance on publishing accessible documents.

If you need help making your document accessible, contact the Directorate of Defence Communications.
